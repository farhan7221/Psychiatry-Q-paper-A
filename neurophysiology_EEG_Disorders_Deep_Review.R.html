Question 1

Which EEG finding is most characteristic of absence seizures?

  • Frontal delta slowing
  • 3 Hz spike-and-wave complexes
  • Occipital alpha attenuation
  • Bilateral burst suppression
Absence seizures, typical of childhood absence epilepsy, are classically associated with generalized 3 Hz spike-and-wave discharges. These occur in a bilaterally synchronous pattern and correlate with brief lapses of awareness. They are often elicited or intensified by hyperventilation. This pattern is highly specific and is a cornerstone in diagnosis.

Question 2

What EEG abnormality is commonly seen in patients with Alzheimer's disease?

  • Focal spike activity
  • Posterior slowing and loss of alpha rhythm
  • Increased beta activity
  • 3 Hz spike-wave
Alzheimer’s disease typically shows diffuse slowing of background rhythms, most prominently as posterior alpha rhythm attenuation. As the disease progresses, theta and delta activity may dominate, indicating cortical dysfunction. These changes reflect the degenerative loss of cholinergic and cortical neurons.

Question 3

Which EEG feature is considered a hallmark of Creutzfeldt-Jakob disease (CJD)?

  • Polyspike bursts
  • Triphasic waves
  • Periodic sharp wave complexes
  • Bilateral frontal beta
CJD is a prion disease characterized on EEG by periodic sharp wave complexes (PSWCs) occurring at 0.5–2 Hz. These high-amplitude sharp waves recur every 1–2 seconds and reflect widespread cortical destruction. This pattern, while not pathognomonic, is highly suggestive when correlated with clinical signs of rapidly progressive dementia and myoclonus.

Question 4

What EEG abnormality may be found in hepatic encephalopathy?

  • Spike-wave discharges
  • Burst suppression
  • Triphasic waves
  • Hypsarrhythmia
Triphasic waves are stereotyped complexes with three phases and a frontal predominance. They are most commonly seen in metabolic encephalopathies like hepatic failure. Although not specific, their presence in the context of altered consciousness supports a metabolic origin.

Question 5

Which EEG finding is common in generalized tonic-clonic seizures postictally?

  • Frontal spikes
  • EEG normalization
  • Diffuse suppression followed by slowing
  • Delta-beta fusion
After a generalized tonic-clonic seizure, the EEG often shows postictal suppression followed by generalized slowing, particularly in delta range. This reflects transient cortical exhaustion and may last several minutes. The pattern helps confirm a generalized seizure when witnessed clinically.

Question 6

What EEG pattern is seen in infantile spasms (West syndrome)?

  • Hypsarrhythmia
  • 3 Hz spike-wave
  • Alpha rhythm excess
  • Triphasic waves
Hypsarrhythmia is a chaotic, high-amplitude, disorganized EEG pattern with multifocal spikes and slow waves. It is a hallmark of West syndrome (infantile spasms) and reflects severe cortical dysfunction. Treatment with ACTH or vigabatrin can improve both clinical and EEG features.

Question 7

Which EEG finding is often observed in patients with ADHD?

  • 3 Hz spike-wave
  • Frontal theta excess
  • Triphasic waves
  • Beta burst suppression
Children with ADHD often show excess frontal-midline theta activity on EEG, reflecting underarousal or immaturity of frontal networks. This is a controversial but consistently observed finding in quantitative EEG (qEEG) studies. It is sometimes used in neurofeedback training aimed at regulating frontal lobe activity.

Question 8

In patients with schizophrenia, which EEG changes are commonly reported?

  • Enhanced alpha coherence
  • Reduced P300 amplitude and increased slow waves
  • Excess sleep spindles
  • Periodic sharp waves
EEG in schizophrenia often reveals decreased P300 amplitude (ERP), reduced alpha coherence, and increased delta/theta slowing. These findings support theories of cortical dysconnectivity and impaired cognitive integration in schizophrenia. Such abnormalities are more prominent in chronic and negative-symptom-dominant subtypes.

Question 9

Which condition is most associated with frontal intermittent rhythmic delta activity (FIRDA)?

  • Generalized epilepsy
  • Temporal lobe epilepsy
  • Encephalopathy or structural brain lesions
  • Narcolepsy
FIRDA consists of rhythmic delta waves (0.5–3 Hz) seen intermittently in the frontal leads. It is a nonspecific marker of diffuse cerebral dysfunction and is often seen in patients with deep midline structural lesions, hydrocephalus, or metabolic encephalopathy.

Question 10

What does generalized periodic discharges (GPDs) on EEG typically indicate?

  • Benign finding
  • Subclinical seizure
  • Severe brain dysfunction or status epilepticus
  • REM sleep onset
GPDs are EEG patterns characterized by repetitive, generalized waveforms at regular intervals. They may reflect ongoing epileptiform activity or post-anoxic injury. When seen in comatose patients, they often indicate a poor prognosis and may require aggressive management to rule out non-convulsive status epilepticus.

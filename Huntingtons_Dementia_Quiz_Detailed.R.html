Huntington’s Dementia Quiz

Prepared by Dr Khalid Farhan

Total Questions: 10

Question 1

What is the primary brain region affected in Huntington’s disease?

  • Cerebellum
  • Frontal cortex
  • Caudate and putamen
  • Thalamus
Huntington’s disease primarily affects the caudate nucleus and putamen, structures within the basal ganglia responsible for motor coordination and cognition. Neuronal loss here causes the characteristic chorea and dementia.

Question 2

What type of dementia is associated with Huntington’s disease?

  • Vascular dementia
  • Frontotemporal dementia
  • Subcortical dementia
  • Cortical dementia
Huntington’s dementia is classified as a subcortical dementia, which presents with slowed cognition, impaired executive function, and emotional dysregulation, often without early memory loss.

Question 3

Which genetic mutation causes Huntington’s disease?

  • Trisomy 21
  • CAG repeat expansion on chromosome 4
  • Tau gene mutation
  • TDP-43 mutation
Huntington’s is caused by a CAG trinucleotide repeat expansion in the HTT gene on chromosome 4. This leads to production of a toxic mutant huntingtin protein.

Question 4

What pathological finding is characteristic of Huntington’s disease on gross brain examination?

  • Cortical thinning
  • Hippocampal sclerosis
  • Dilation of lateral ventricles
  • Cerebellar atrophy
Grossly, there is atrophy of the caudate nuclei and compensatory dilation of the lateral ventricles, creating the classic 'ex vacuo' hydrocephalus appearance.

Question 5

What type of neuronal inclusion is found in Huntington’s disease?

  • Lewy bodies
  • Pick bodies
  • Tau tangles
  • Intranuclear inclusions with huntingtin
Mutant huntingtin protein accumulates in the nuclei of neurons, forming characteristic intranuclear inclusions which are toxic and interfere with normal cell function.

Question 6

What is the normal function of huntingtin protein?

  • Myelin synthesis
  • Synaptic pruning
  • Axonal transport and vesicle trafficking
  • Tau stabilization
The normal huntingtin protein is involved in vesicle transport, endocytosis, and regulation of transcription. Mutation disrupts these functions and leads to neurotoxicity.

Question 7

Which brain imaging feature supports a diagnosis of Huntington’s disease?

  • White matter demyelination
  • Ventricular narrowing
  • Frontal lobe hyperintensity
  • Caudate atrophy with ventricular enlargement
Neuroimaging often shows atrophy of the caudate nuclei and putamen, leading to enlargement of the frontal horns of the lateral ventricles.

Question 8

Which motor symptom is most characteristic of Huntington’s disease?

  • Tremor
  • Chorea
  • Rigidity
  • Ataxia
Chorea, a form of involuntary, irregular movement, is a hallmark of Huntington’s disease, reflecting basal ganglia dysfunction.

Question 9

What psychiatric symptom commonly precedes cognitive or motor changes in Huntington’s?

  • Delusions
  • Anxiety
  • Depression
  • Hallucinations
Depression and mood disturbances are often early manifestations in Huntington’s, sometimes occurring years before motor symptoms.

Question 10

What inheritance pattern does Huntington’s disease follow?

  • Autosomal recessive
  • Mitochondrial
  • Autosomal dominant
  • X-linked dominant
Huntington’s disease follows an autosomal dominant inheritance pattern. Each child of an affected individual has a 50% chance of inheriting the mutation.

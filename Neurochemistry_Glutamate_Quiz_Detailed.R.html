Neurochemistry of Glutamate Quiz

Prepared by Dr Khalid Farhan

Total Questions: 10

Question 1

What is the primary role of glutamate in the CNS?

  • Inhibitory neurotransmitter
  • Excitatory neurotransmitter
  • Neuromodulator only
  • Hormone
Glutamate is the main excitatory neurotransmitter in the CNS, mediating most fast synaptic transmission and critical for learning and memory.

Question 2

Which precursor molecule is essential for glutamate synthesis?

  • Aspartate
  • Alpha-ketoglutarate
  • Tyrosine
  • GABA
Glutamate is synthesized from alpha-ketoglutarate, a Krebs cycle intermediate, by transamination reactions in neurons.

Question 3

Which enzyme converts glutamate to GABA?

  • Glutamine synthetase
  • Glutamate decarboxylase (GAD)
  • Glutamate dehydrogenase
  • Aspartate transaminase
Glutamate decarboxylase (GAD) converts glutamate to GABA, linking excitatory and inhibitory neurotransmission pathways.

Question 4

Which receptor subtype is a ligand-gated ion channel for glutamate?

  • AMPA
  • mGluR1
  • GABA-A
  • Muscarinic
AMPA receptors are ionotropic glutamate receptors that mediate fast excitatory postsynaptic currents by allowing sodium influx.

Question 5

Which receptor subtype is G-protein-coupled for glutamate?

  • AMPA
  • NMDA
  • Kainate
  • mGluR (metabotropic)
Metabotropic glutamate receptors (mGluRs) are G-protein-coupled and modulate neuronal excitability and synaptic plasticity.

Question 6

What ion is essential for NMDA receptor activation?

  • Sodium
  • Potassium
  • Calcium
  • Chloride
NMDA receptors are permeable to calcium and require both ligand binding and membrane depolarization to remove magnesium block and allow calcium influx.

Question 7

Which brain function is heavily dependent on glutamatergic neurotransmission?

  • Hormone secretion
  • Visual processing
  • Memory and learning
  • Autonomic control
Glutamate is crucial for synaptic plasticity mechanisms like long-term potentiation (LTP), underlying learning and memory processes.

Question 8

Which transporter clears glutamate from the synaptic cleft?

  • Vesicular glutamate transporter (VGLUT)
  • Excitatory amino acid transporter (EAAT)
  • Glycine transporter
  • Dopamine transporter
Excitatory amino acid transporters (EAATs), located on neurons and glial cells, clear glutamate from the synapse to prevent excitotoxicity.

Question 9

Excessive glutamate activity can cause what pathological effect?

  • Hypoexcitability
  • Excitotoxicity
  • Demyelination
  • Neuroprotection
Overactivation of glutamate receptors leads to excitotoxicity, causing calcium overload and neuronal injury, implicated in stroke and neurodegenerative diseases.

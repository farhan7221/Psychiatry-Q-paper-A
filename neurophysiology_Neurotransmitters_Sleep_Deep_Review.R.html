Question 1

Which neurotransmitter plays a key role in initiating REM sleep?

  • Serotonin
  • GABA
  • Acetylcholine
  • Histamine
Acetylcholine (ACh) is the primary neurotransmitter responsible for initiating and maintaining REM sleep. Cholinergic neurons in the pons become highly active during REM, especially in the later part of the night. This activation leads to cortical desynchronization, muscle atonia, and rapid eye movements. Cholinergic activation is also associated with vivid dreaming. Drugs that increase cholinergic tone (like physostigmine) can enhance REM, while anticholinergics suppress it.

Question 2

Which neurotransmitter system is crucial for maintaining wakefulness and is deficient in narcolepsy?

  • Serotonin
  • Dopamine
  • Orexin
  • GABA
Orexin (also known as hypocretin) is a neuropeptide produced in the lateral hypothalamus and plays a vital role in stabilizing wakefulness. It acts on multiple arousal centers including noradrenergic, histaminergic, and dopaminergic systems. In narcolepsy with cataplexy, there is a selective loss of orexin-producing neurons, leading to sudden REM intrusions and fragmented wake-sleep transitions. Orexin receptor agonists (e.g., lemborexant) are now used therapeutically to promote balanced sleep-wake states.

Question 3

What is the role of serotonin in the sleep-wake cycle?

  • Suppresses REM and promotes sleep onset
  • Initiates REM
  • Blocks NREM
  • Induces wakefulness
Serotonin (5-HT), especially via the raphe nuclei, plays a dual role in sleep. It promotes sleep onset (especially NREM) and suppresses REM sleep early in the night. However, it is also necessary for the buildup of REM pressure, indirectly regulating REM later. Low serotonin is linked with insomnia and depression. SSRIs suppress REM and increase latency, which may correlate with clinical improvement in depression.

Question 4

Which neurotransmitter promotes alertness and is targeted by antihistamines to induce drowsiness?

  • Dopamine
  • Histamine
  • Serotonin
  • GABA
Histamine, particularly through H1 receptors in the tuberomammillary nucleus (TMN), promotes cortical arousal and wakefulness. Antihistamines (especially first-generation) cross the blood-brain barrier and block H1 receptors, resulting in sedation and drowsiness. This mechanism is utilized in over-the-counter sleep aids. In contrast, H3 antagonists enhance histamine release and promote wakefulness.

Question 5

Which inhibitory neurotransmitter is primarily responsible for sleep maintenance?

  • Glutamate
  • Dopamine
  • Serotonin
  • GABA
GABA (gamma-aminobutyric acid) is the main inhibitory neurotransmitter in the CNS and is essential for sleep maintenance. GABAergic neurons in the ventrolateral preoptic nucleus (VLPO) inhibit arousal systems (histamine, norepinephrine, orexin), facilitating NREM sleep. Most sleep-promoting drugs (benzodiazepines, Z-drugs) enhance GABAergic signaling at GABA-A receptors to prolong sleep duration and reduce awakenings.

Question 6

Which neurotransmitter's activity increases during REM sleep, contributing to vivid dreams?

  • Histamine
  • Acetylcholine
  • GABA
  • Norepinephrine
Acetylcholine activity surges during REM sleep, especially from pontine tegmentum neurons. This cholinergic activation leads to cortical excitation, rapid eye movement, and vivid dream experiences. It also contributes to REM-related muscle atonia via medullary mechanisms. REM is often absent or shortened with anticholinergic medications.

Question 7

How do norepinephrine levels change during REM sleep?

  • They increase
  • They remain constant
  • They sharply decrease
  • They fluctuate randomly
Norepinephrine levels drop sharply during REM sleep. Locus coeruleus neurons become nearly silent during this phase, reducing arousal tone and allowing for REM-specific neural processing. This noradrenergic silence is critical for REM atonia and may explain impaired memory encoding during REM. Reactivation of norepinephrine post-REM contributes to awakening or transitioning to NREM.

Question 8

What is the combined effect of serotonin and acetylcholine on the sleep cycle?

  • They both promote REM sleep equally
  • Serotonin inhibits REM while acetylcholine initiates it
  • Both suppress deep sleep
  • They synchronize circadian rhythm
Serotonin and acetylcholine have opposing roles in REM regulation. Serotonin from the raphe nuclei inhibits REM onset early in the night, while acetylcholine initiates and maintains REM later. This balance allows for proper sequencing of NREM and REM stages across the night. Antidepressants often modulate this interaction by increasing serotonergic tone.

Question 9

Which neurotransmitter system is activated by stimulants like amphetamines and modafinil to promote wakefulness?

  • GABAergic
  • Cholinergic
  • Monoaminergic (dopamine/norepinephrine)
  • Glutamatergic
Stimulants like amphetamines and modafinil increase dopamine and norepinephrine availability in the CNS. They activate the monoaminergic arousal systems, particularly in the locus coeruleus and ventral tegmental area (VTA), resulting in increased wakefulness, attention, and reduced REM. These agents are used in conditions like narcolepsy and ADHD.

Question 10

What is the impact of anticholinergic drugs on sleep architecture?

  • Increase REM duration
  • Improve deep sleep
  • Suppress REM sleep
  • Increase sleep spindles
Anticholinergic medications (e.g., diphenhydramine, tricyclic antidepressants) suppress REM sleep by inhibiting cholinergic transmission. They may increase sleep latency and reduce REM duration, often leading to fragmented sleep. Chronic use can impair memory consolidation and dream recall. Clinically, anticholinergic burden is a concern in the elderly due to associated cognitive effects.

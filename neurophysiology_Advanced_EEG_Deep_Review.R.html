Question 1

What does MEG (Magnetoencephalography) primarily measure?

  • Electrical activity of the brain
  • Magnetic fields generated by neural activity
  • Blood oxygenation
  • Ionic calcium influx
MEG detects the tiny magnetic fields produced by neuronal electrical currents, primarily from pyramidal neurons in the cortex. Unlike EEG, which measures voltage differences across the scalp, MEG is more sensitive to tangential currents and offers higher spatial resolution. It is useful in localizing epileptic foci and studying brain networks non-invasively. MEG is often combined with MRI (as in MR-EEG fusion) for precise mapping.

Question 2

Which advanced EEG technique allows precise estimation of the cortical origin of recorded signals?

  • Polysomnography
  • Source localization
  • Actigraphy
  • ERP averaging
Source localization uses mathematical models (like dipole fitting and inverse solutions) to estimate where in the brain an EEG signal originates. It enhances the clinical utility of EEG in epilepsy surgery planning and research on cortical dynamics. Techniques such as LORETA (Low Resolution Brain Electromagnetic Tomography) and sLORETA are widely used.

Question 3

What is the main advantage of high-density EEG systems (e.g., 128 or 256 electrodes)?

  • Lower noise
  • Improved spatial resolution
  • Enhanced blood flow imaging
  • Faster sampling rates
High-density EEG provides much finer spatial resolution by covering the scalp with a large number of electrodes. This allows more accurate mapping of cortical activity and improves the precision of source localization. It is increasingly used in research settings and epilepsy evaluation, but setup and analysis are more complex.

Question 4

Which application uses EEG signals to control external devices based on brain activity?

  • ERP mapping
  • Magnetoencephalography
  • Brain-computer interface (BCI)
  • Quantitative EEG
Brain-computer interfaces (BCIs) translate specific EEG patterns (like motor imagery or P300 responses) into control signals for external devices such as prosthetics, communication aids, or computers. BCIs have revolutionized assistive technologies for patients with paralysis or locked-in syndrome. They rely on real-time EEG decoding and feedback training.

Question 5

What is quantitative EEG (qEEG) primarily used for?

  • Routine epilepsy diagnosis
  • Measuring blood flow
  • Statistical analysis of EEG patterns
  • Detecting calcium influx
qEEG involves digitizing EEG data and applying mathematical/statistical algorithms to analyze frequency, coherence, symmetry, and power. It allows objective assessment of cortical rhythms and has applications in neurofeedback, brain mapping, and research into disorders like ADHD and dementia. While useful, qEEG must be interpreted cautiously and is not a replacement for clinical EEG.

Question 6

In which context is the P300 speller used as part of a BCI system?

  • Cognitive therapy
  • Communication for paralyzed patients
  • Sleep disorder diagnosis
  • Pain monitoring
The P300 speller is a BCI application that allows individuals to select letters or commands by focusing attention on a flashing matrix. The P300 wave, an ERP component elicited by target stimuli, is detected and decoded to identify the user’s intent. It has been especially transformative for patients with ALS or severe motor disabilities.

Question 7

What is the key limitation of MEG compared to EEG?

  • Poor temporal resolution
  • Limited spatial resolution
  • High cost and limited availability
  • Cannot detect cortical activity
MEG offers excellent temporal and spatial resolution but is extremely expensive and requires shielded rooms and specialized equipment. Its high cost and technical demands limit its widespread clinical use. EEG, while lower in spatial resolution, remains the standard for routine brain monitoring.

Question 8

Which technique combines EEG with functional MRI to map both electrical and hemodynamic brain responses?

  • EEG-fNIRS
  • MEG
  • EEG-fMRI
  • Actigraphy
EEG-fMRI combines the millisecond precision of EEG with the spatial accuracy of fMRI. This hybrid approach allows researchers to study the relationship between fast neural activity and slower blood oxygenation changes. It is useful in epilepsy research, cognitive neuroscience, and investigating neurovascular coupling.

Question 9

Which EEG feature is most relevant in real-time seizure prediction systems?

  • Triphasic waves
  • Alpha peak frequency
  • Preictal EEG patterns and synchronization shifts
  • Eye blink artifacts
Advanced seizure prediction algorithms analyze subtle EEG changes leading up to seizures, known as preictal patterns. These include shifts in synchronization, spectral power, and connectivity. Accurate prediction can enable timely interventions, though clinical deployment is still under development.

Question 10

What is an advantage of dry-electrode EEG systems over conventional wet systems?

  • Higher impedance
  • Easier and faster setup
  • Better frequency resolution
  • Enhanced deep brain recording
Dry-electrode EEG systems avoid conductive gels, making them quicker and more convenient to set up. They are increasingly used in portable EEG, sports monitoring, and consumer-grade neurofeedback. However, they may have higher impedance and slightly lower signal quality compared to wet electrodes.

Question 1

Which component of the Visual Evoked Potential (VEP) is most commonly used clinically?

  • N70
  • P100
  • N200
  • P300
The P100 component of the VEP is a positive wave occurring approximately 100 milliseconds after visual stimulus onset. It is highly consistent and symmetrical in healthy individuals, making it reliable for assessing the integrity of the visual pathways. A delay or asymmetry in P100 latency is a hallmark of optic nerve demyelination, especially in multiple sclerosis.

Question 2

What is the clinical utility of Brainstem Auditory Evoked Potentials (BAEPs)?

  • Evaluating seizure onset
  • Testing cortical function
  • Assessing brainstem integrity
  • Monitoring visual cortex
BAEPs (or BSAEPs) are generated by presenting brief auditory clicks and recording neural responses from the cochlea to the midbrain. They are composed of seven waves (I–VII), each representing a relay in the auditory pathway. They are especially useful in assessing hearing in infants and in detecting brainstem lesions, tumors, or coma-related dysfunction.

Question 3

Which wave of the BAEP reflects auditory nerve activity?

  • Wave I
  • Wave III
  • Wave V
  • Wave VII
Wave I of the BAEP is generated by the auditory nerve (cranial nerve VIII) and reflects the earliest neural response to sound stimuli. It is followed by subsequent waves from cochlear nucleus (II), superior olivary complex (III), and so on. Wave I is crucial for assessing peripheral auditory function and differentiating between conductive and sensorineural hearing loss.

Question 4

In Somatosensory Evoked Potentials (SEPs), what does the N20 component indicate?

  • Brainstem damage
  • Cortical somatosensory response
  • Spinal reflex loop
  • Peripheral nerve block
N20 is a cortical potential recorded from the contralateral somatosensory cortex in response to median nerve stimulation. It reflects central somatosensory pathway conduction, from the brachial plexus through the spinal cord and thalamus to the cortex. N20 latency prolongation or absence is used to assess dorsal column and brainstem dysfunction, particularly in spinal cord injury and coma prognosis.

Question 5

Which modality is most affected in patients with multiple sclerosis?

  • VEP
  • BAEP
  • EMG
  • MEG
Multiple sclerosis frequently affects the optic nerves due to demyelination. This leads to delayed P100 latency in VEP testing, even when clinical symptoms are mild or subclinical. VEPs are a sensitive tool for detecting early demyelination and can support MS diagnosis when combined with MRI and CSF analysis.

Question 6

What is a common use of SEPs during surgery?

  • Induce anesthesia
  • Measure cerebral perfusion
  • Monitor spinal cord integrity
  • Predict dreaming
SEPs are widely used intraoperatively during spine and brain surgeries to monitor the functional integrity of somatosensory pathways. Significant changes in amplitude or latency during surgery may indicate potential spinal cord injury, prompting immediate intervention.

Question 7

Which evoked potential is most useful in assessing hearing in newborns?

  • VEP
  • P300
  • BAEP
  • N100
BAEPs are non-invasive, objective, and do not require behavioral responses, making them ideal for assessing auditory function in newborns and infants. Normal waveforms indicate intact peripheral and brainstem auditory pathways, and abnormal responses can guide early intervention for hearing loss.

Question 8

How are evoked potentials useful in comatose patients?

  • Diagnose epilepsy
  • Detect brain death
  • Assess sensory pathway integrity
  • Monitor eye movements
Evoked potentials like BAEPs and SEPs are valuable in evaluating residual brain function in comatose patients. Preserved responses indicate intact brainstem or cortical pathways and may carry prognostic significance. Their absence, especially of cortical components like N20, is associated with poor outcomes after anoxic injury.

Question 9

Which evoked potential is least influenced by sedation or anesthesia?

  • VEP
  • P300
  • BAEP
  • N170
BAEPs are remarkably resistant to sedation and general anesthesia, as they reflect subcortical processing in the brainstem. This makes them ideal for intraoperative monitoring and in unresponsive patients. In contrast, cortical potentials (e.g., VEPs, P300) are more affected by CNS depressants.

Question 10

Which ERP component is associated with detection of novel auditory stimuli without conscious attention?

  • MMN
  • P300
  • CNV
  • VEP-N100
Mismatch negativity (MMN) is an ERP component elicited when an auditory deviation is detected in a regular sound sequence. It does not require active attention, making it a tool for assessing pre-attentive auditory processing. It is often reduced in schizophrenia and cognitive disorders, supporting its use in research and diagnosis.

HIV-associated Pathology Quiz

Prepared by Dr Khalid Farhan

Total Questions: 10

Question 1

Which cells are the primary targets for HIV entry into the CNS?

  • Astrocytes
  • Neurons
  • CD4+ T cells and monocytes
  • Endothelial cells
CD4+ T lymphocytes and monocytes are the main cells involved in HIV entry and CNS invasion, with monocytes playing a crucial role in trafficking the virus across the blood-brain barrier.

Question 2

What is the 'Trojan Horse' mechanism of HIV CNS entry?

  • Free virus entry through endothelial pores
  • HIV crosses BBB within infected immune cells
  • Migration via CSF circulation
  • Infection of neurons directly
The 'Trojan Horse' hypothesis suggests that HIV crosses the blood-brain barrier (BBB) by hitching a ride inside infected monocytes or T cells, which later release the virus into the CNS.

Question 3

Which HIV strain characteristic increases macrophage infection in the brain?

  • CCR5 suppression
  • gp120 mutation favoring macrophage-tropism
  • CD8+ stimulation
  • Neuronal binding
Macrophage-tropic strains of HIV arise due to mutations in gp120, enhancing their ability to infect monocytes/macrophages rather than T lymphocytes.

Question 4

What are the two main mechanisms of HIV-associated neurodegeneration?

  • Direct HIV entry and neuronal apoptosis
  • Astrocyte infiltration and tauopathy
  • Direct infection of neurons and B cell inflammation
  • Cytokine release and viral protein neurotoxicity
Neurodegeneration is caused by viral proteins (Tat, gp120) and excessive pro-inflammatory cytokine release by infected microglia and macrophages, leading to synaptic and neuronal damage.

Question 5

Which of the following is NOT a common histological finding in HIV-related brain pathology?

  • Microglial nodules
  • Multinucleated giant cells
  • Lewy bodies
  • Astrocytosis
Lewy bodies are not a feature of HIV neuropathology. Instead, microglial nodules, multinucleated giant cells, and reactive astrocytes are commonly seen.

Question 6

Which region shows significant neuronal loss in HIV-associated dementia?

  • Occipital cortex
  • Basal ganglia and hippocampus
  • Spinal cord
  • Cerebellum
Neuronal damage is especially notable in the hippocampus, basal ganglia, and caudate nucleus, contributing to cognitive and motor deficits.

Question 7

What is the most common psychiatric presentation in AIDS-related dementia?

  • Psychosis
  • Seizures
  • Depression
  • Euphoria
Depression is the most common psychiatric symptom in HIV-associated dementia, occurring more frequently than psychosis, which is relatively rare.

Question 8

Which factor contributes to blood-brain barrier disruption in HIV?

  • Viral capsid degradation
  • TNF-alpha and cytokines
  • Oligodendrocyte infection
  • Neuron loss only
TNF-alpha and other pro-inflammatory cytokines released by infected microglia and macrophages weaken the integrity of the blood-brain barrier, allowing more viral and immune cell entry.

Question 9

Which viral load compartment is less reliable for diagnosing CNS HIV disease?

  • CSF viral load
  • Plasma viral load
  • Tonsillar tissue
  • Spleen viral RNA
Although HIV RNA can be detected in CSF, CSF viral load is not a reliable predictor of CNS disease severity or progression.

Question 10

Which biopsy feature is characteristic of advanced HIV encephalopathy?

  • Amyloid plaques
  • Axonal spheroids
  • Accumulation of lipid-laden macrophages
  • Tau-positive inclusions
In severe HIV encephalopathy, brain tissue often shows lipid-laden macrophages, microglial nodules, and demyelination, especially in white matter.

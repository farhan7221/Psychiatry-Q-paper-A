Question 1

At what membrane potential do sodium (Na⁺) channels open to initiate an action potential?

  • -40 mV
  • -55 mV
  • +30 mV
  • 0 mV
The threshold for action potential initiation is typically around -55 mV. When the resting membrane potential depolarizes from -70 mV to -55 mV, voltage-gated sodium channels at the axon hillock open, allowing a rapid influx of Na⁺, which initiates the action potential.

Question 2

What is the peak membrane potential reached during an action potential?

  • 0 mV
  • +20 mV
  • +40 mV
  • +70 mV
During the depolarization phase, continued Na⁺ influx reverses the membrane potential up to +40 mV. This is the peak before repolarization begins, marking the full reversal of the neuron’s resting potential.

Question 3

Which channels open during the repolarization phase of an action potential?

  • Calcium channels
  • Sodium channels
  • Potassium channels
  • Chloride channels
After the peak of the action potential, voltage-gated potassium (K⁺) channels open, allowing K⁺ to exit the neuron. This outflow repolarizes the membrane back toward the resting potential.

Question 4

Where is an action potential typically initiated?

  • Soma
  • Dendrites
  • Axon hillock
  • Synaptic cleft
The axon hillock, also known as the initial segment, has the highest density of voltage-gated sodium channels. This makes it the ideal site for the summation of excitatory inputs and initiation of action potentials.

Question 5

What is the main distinction between chemical and electrical synapses?

  • Electrical synapses release neurotransmitters
  • Chemical synapses allow direct ion flow
  • Chemical synapses use neurotransmitters; electrical synapses use gap junctions
  • Chemical synapses are faster
Chemical synapses involve neurotransmitter release across a synaptic cleft, while electrical synapses involve direct ionic current flow via gap junctions, allowing cells to synchronize their activity.

Question 6

Which type of synapse is typically faster: chemical or electrical?

  • Electrical synapse
  • Chemical synapse
  • Both equal
  • Depends on neurotransmitter
Electrical synapses transmit signals almost instantaneously through gap junctions, making them faster than chemical synapses, which involve multiple steps like vesicle release and receptor binding.

Question 7

What is the term for enhanced postsynaptic potential due to multiple excitatory synapses firing at once?

  • Temporal summation
  • Spatial summation
  • Facilitation
  • Inhibition
Spatial summation occurs when multiple presynaptic neurons release neurotransmitters at different synapses simultaneously, causing a combined effect that can trigger an action potential in the postsynaptic neuron.

Question 8

What is facilitation in synaptic transmission?

  • Reduction of response
  • Blocking of synaptic vesicles
  • Enhancement of synaptic strength due to prior activity
  • Closure of ion channels
Facilitation is a form of short-term synaptic plasticity where repeated stimulation enhances neurotransmitter release, usually due to residual calcium buildup in the presynaptic terminal.

Question 9

Which synapse type has both electrical and chemical properties?

  • Electrical synapse
  • Chemical synapse
  • Conjoint synapse
  • Axo-axonic synapse
Conjoint synapses have characteristics of both electrical and chemical synapses, allowing them to modulate signal transmission with both direct ionic flow and neurotransmitter release.

Question 10

What is the primary ion responsible for depolarization during an action potential?

  • Calcium (Ca²⁺)
  • Potassium (K⁺)
  • Sodium (Na⁺)
  • Chloride (Cl⁻)
Sodium ions are key to depolarization. When voltage-gated Na⁺ channels open, Na⁺ rapidly enters the neuron, making the inside more positive and initiating the rising phase of the action potential.

Question 1

Which EEG wave is most dominant when a person is awake but relaxed with eyes closed?

  • Beta
  • Theta
  • Alpha
  • Delta
Alpha waves (8–13 Hz) are typically observed in the occipital and parietal regions of the brain. They represent a relaxed wakeful state and are most prominent when the eyes are closed. Generated by thalamocortical circuits, they reflect an idling rhythm of the cortex. Opening the eyes, or engaging in cognitive tasks, leads to suppression of these waves—a response known as alpha blocking. This phenomenon is used clinically to assess cortical responsiveness and integrity.

Question 2

What EEG wave appears most prominently during focused attention or anxiety?

  • Delta
  • Alpha
  • Theta
  • Beta
Beta waves (>13 Hz) are high-frequency, low-amplitude brain waves associated with active concentration, anxiety, and motor activity. They are most commonly found in the frontal-central regions and increase with alertness, thinking, or stress. In clinical EEG, excessive beta activity may be observed in patients with anxiety or during pharmacologic states such as benzodiazepine or stimulant use. These waves also have significance in sensorimotor rhythm (SMR) biofeedback protocols.

Question 3

Which wave has a frequency below 4 Hz and is normally seen in deep sleep (Stage 3–4 NREM)?

  • Alpha
  • Mu
  • Delta
  • Theta
Delta waves are the slowest EEG waves, with frequencies <4 Hz and high amplitudes. These waves predominate in slow-wave sleep (SWS), specifically stages 3 and 4 of NREM sleep. Delta activity signifies synchronized cortical function important for physical recovery, immune balance, and hormone secretion (e.g., growth hormone). In wakefulness, prominent delta waves are pathological and may indicate encephalopathy, brain lesions, or deep white matter disease.

Question 4

The Mu wave is seen over which brain region and is related to what function?

  • Frontal lobe, speech
  • Occipital lobe, vision
  • Motor cortex, movement
  • Temporal lobe, memory
Mu waves (7–11 Hz) resemble alpha waves in frequency and shape but originate from the sensorimotor cortex. They are suppressed ('blocked') during voluntary movement or even when observing someone else move. This suppression is linked to mirror neuron systems and is used in research related to autism spectrum disorders and motor rehabilitation. Mu rhythm reactivity is also useful in brain-computer interface (BCI) technologies.

Question 5

Which of the following waves disappears with eye opening?

  • Beta
  • Alpha
  • Theta
  • Delta
Alpha wave suppression following eye opening is called alpha blocking. It is mediated by cholinergic activation of the thalamus and cortex, which interrupts rhythmic firing and promotes desynchronization. This response is used in routine EEGs as a test of brain reactivity. Lack of alpha suppression can suggest poor arousal, as seen in toxic-metabolic encephalopathies or coma.

Question 6

What is the clinical significance of excessive theta waves in an awake adult?

  • Normal finding
  • Marker of intelligence
  • Sign of cerebral dysfunction
  • Indicates REM sleep
Theta waves (4–8 Hz) are common during drowsiness and light sleep (Stage 1). In an awake adult, especially if widespread and sustained, they may indicate diffuse cortical dysfunction. Conditions like hepatic encephalopathy, traumatic brain injury, or drug intoxication can lead to excessive theta. In children, theta waves are more prevalent and often physiologic.

Question 7

Lambda waves are typically seen in what state and region?

  • During REM sleep in frontal region
  • During visual scanning in occipital region
  • In deep sleep over parietal region
  • In epilepsy over temporal region
Lambda waves are small, sharp transients seen in the occipital leads during active visual exploration (e.g., reading or scanning images). They are benign, occur with eyes open, and are often seen in children and young adults. Importantly, they can be mistaken for epileptiform discharges, so clinical correlation is essential.

Question 8

Which two EEG waves are typically classified as 'slow waves'?

  • Beta and alpha
  • Alpha and theta
  • Theta and delta
  • Mu and lambda
Theta (4–8 Hz) and Delta (<4 Hz) are slow waves associated with decreased cortical arousal and synchrony of neuronal activity. They dominate NREM sleep and are used to quantify sleep depth in polysomnography. In pathologic states, they can appear during wakefulness and suggest cortical dysfunction.

Question 9

In which population are Delta and Theta waves the most dominant EEG pattern?

  • Adults
  • Infants
  • Newborns
  • Adolescents
The immature brain of a newborn exhibits predominantly delta and theta activity, which is physiologic and expected. The EEG evolves with age to include faster rhythms such as alpha and beta. Monitoring EEG maturation helps identify developmental delays or neonatal encephalopathies.

Question 10

What change occurs in EEG alpha wave dominance with aging?

  • It increases
  • It remains unchanged
  • It reduces
  • It turns into beta waves
In elderly individuals, alpha wave amplitude decreases, and the rhythm becomes slower and less reactive. There is often an increase in background theta or delta, especially in cognitive decline. Tracking alpha activity is useful in diagnosing dementias, where slowing of alpha is an early electrophysiological sign.

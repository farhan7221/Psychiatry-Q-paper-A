Question 31

What are Hirano bodies composed of?

  • Neurofilaments
  • Tubulin
  • Actin and actin-associated proteins
  • Tau protein
Hirano bodies are rod-shaped, eosinophilic intracellular inclusions composed of actin and actin-associated proteins. They are typically found in the pyramidal neurons of the hippocampus. Their presence reflects cellular stress and degeneration, and although not specific, they are often seen in Alzheimer's disease and other neurodegenerative disorders.

Question 32

Where are Hirano bodies most frequently seen in the brain?

  • Cerebellum
  • Brainstem
  • Hippocampus
  • Occipital lobe
The hippocampus is a key structure for memory encoding and retrieval, and Hirano bodies are most frequently observed in its pyramidal cells, particularly in the CA1 region. Their presence in this area reinforces the link between hippocampal pathology and cognitive impairment in Alzheimer’s disease.

Question 33

Which of the following most closely correlates with cognitive decline in Alzheimer’s?

  • Neuritic plaques
  • Presence of Hirano bodies
  • Gliosis
  • Number of neurofibrillary tangles
The most reliable neuropathological correlate of cognitive decline in Alzheimer’s is the number and distribution of neurofibrillary tangles, particularly in the neocortex. While plaques are also present, their correlation with clinical symptoms is weaker. Even a small number of tangles in critical cortical areas can signify marked cognitive dysfunction.

Question 34

What is synaptophysin used to identify in Alzheimer’s pathology?

  • Amyloid plaques
  • Tau tangles
  • Synapses
  • Glial cells
Synaptophysin is a glycoprotein found in the membranes of presynaptic vesicles. In Alzheimer’s disease, synapse loss is a significant contributor to cognitive decline. Immunohistochemistry using anti-synaptophysin antibodies allows quantification of synaptic density, serving as a useful marker in research and neuropathology.

Question 35

Which hippocampal structure shows prominent neuronal loss in Alzheimer’s?

  • CA1 only
  • CA3 and dentate gyrus
  • Subiculum and layers II & IV of entorhinal cortex
  • Amygdala
Histopathological studies in Alzheimer’s consistently reveal prominent neuronal loss in the subiculum and layers II and IV of the entorhinal cortex. These regions form part of the hippocampal formation and play essential roles in processing and relaying memory-related information to and from the neocortex. Their degeneration leads to early memory impairment.

Question 36

What is the function of the entorhinal cortex affected in Alzheimer’s disease?

  • Vision
  • Olfaction
  • Memory integration
  • Language processing
The entorhinal cortex is a critical relay station between the neocortex and hippocampus, forming part of the medial temporal lobe memory system. It contributes to the formation, consolidation, and spatial mapping of episodic memory. Damage to this area is an early finding in Alzheimer’s disease and closely linked to initial memory deficits.

Question 37

What is the hallmark pathology of Cerebral Amyloid Angiopathy (CAA)?

  • Tau tangle deposition
  • Amyloid-beta accumulation in vessel walls
  • Neurofibrillary degeneration
  • Lewy body inclusions
Cerebral Amyloid Angiopathy (CAA) involves the accumulation of beta-amyloid within the walls of small to medium-sized cerebral arteries and arterioles. These deposits compromise the integrity of vessel walls, increasing the risk of hemorrhage and contributing to cognitive dysfunction. CAA frequently coexists with Alzheimer’s pathology.

Question 38

What kind of stroke is most commonly associated with Cerebral Amyloid Angiopathy?

  • Ischemic stroke
  • Lobar hemorrhage
  • Lacunar infarct
  • Subdural hematoma
CAA is a leading cause of lobar hemorrhages in elderly individuals. The amyloid deposition weakens the walls of superficial cortical vessels, leading to their rupture and resulting in hemorrhages that are typically superficial but can extend into subarachnoid spaces. These events are distinct from deep hypertensive bleeds seen in basal ganglia or thalamus.

Question 39

What percentage of Alzheimer’s patients have evidence of CAA?

  • 20%
  • 40%
  • Over 90%
  • 60%
Studies have shown that over 90% of individuals with Alzheimer’s disease exhibit some degree of CAA, with more extensive and severe vascular involvement than age-matched controls. This comorbidity is thought to accelerate disease progression and contribute to additional clinical features, such as microbleeds or stroke-like symptoms.

Question 40

What is Binswanger’s disease best described as?

  • Alzheimer’s variant
  • Lewy body dementia
  • Subcortical vascular dementia
  • Cortical laminar necrosis
Binswanger’s disease, or subcortical arteriosclerotic encephalopathy, is a type of vascular dementia caused by chronic hypertension and small vessel disease. It is characterized by white matter demyelination and small infarcts that spare cortical gray matter. It often coexists with Alzheimer-type pathology in elderly patients, complicating diagnosis and management.

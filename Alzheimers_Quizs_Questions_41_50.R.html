Alzheimer’s Quizs

Prepared by Dr Khalid Farhan

Total Questions: 10 (41–50)

Question 41

Which enzyme prevents the formation of Aβ peptide in the non-amyloidogenic pathway?

  • Beta-secretase
  • Gamma-secretase
  • Alpha-secretase
  • Caspase
In the non-amyloidogenic pathway, alpha-secretase cleaves APP within the Aβ domain, preventing Aβ peptide formation. This is considered a protective mechanism against Alzheimer’s pathology.

Question 42

What is the role of gamma-secretase in Alzheimer’s disease?

  • Forms tau aggregates
  • Cleaves APP after beta-secretase to produce Aβ
  • Degrades synaptic vesicles
  • Synthesizes myelin
Gamma-secretase works with beta-secretase to cleave APP and release Aβ peptides. Mutations in its components (like presenilin 1 and 2) are linked to familial Alzheimer’s.

Question 43

What neuropathological change is primarily responsible for early memory loss in Alzheimer’s?

  • Amyloid in cerebellum
  • White matter infarcts
  • Hippocampal neurodegeneration
  • Ventricular dilation
The hippocampus, essential for memory formation, is affected early in Alzheimer’s with neuronal loss and synaptic dysfunction, leading to memory impairment.

Question 44

What does “amyloid cascade hypothesis” propose?

  • Tau protein triggers amyloid accumulation
  • Aβ deposition initiates downstream pathology
  • Glial inflammation causes primary damage
  • Vascular ischemia starts neurodegeneration
The amyloid cascade hypothesis posits that Aβ accumulation is the primary trigger in Alzheimer’s, leading to tau pathology, inflammation, synaptic failure, and neuronal death.

Question 45

What is granulovacuolar degeneration?

  • Aggregates of myelin debris
  • Small vacuoles with granules inside neurons
  • Calcium deposits in axons
  • Mitochondrial swelling
Granulovacuolar degeneration refers to cytoplasmic vacuoles with granules found in neurons, especially in hippocampal pyramidal cells. It is often seen alongside other Alzheimer’s changes.

Question 46

What is the pathological hallmark of advanced Alzheimer’s disease?

  • Mild plaque formation
  • Astrocyte hypertrophy
  • Extensive neurofibrillary tangles and cortical atrophy
  • Vascular thrombosis
In late-stage Alzheimer’s, widespread tangles, severe synapse loss, and cortical thinning dominate, especially in temporal and parietal regions.

Question 47

Which protein is found in Hirano bodies?

  • Tau
  • Actin
  • Ubiquitin
  • TDP-43
Hirano bodies are composed primarily of actin and actin-binding proteins. These rod-shaped inclusions are often observed in neurodegenerative conditions like Alzheimer’s.

Question 48

Which structure connects the hippocampus to the neocortex?

  • Fornix
  • Corpus callosum
  • Entorhinal cortex
  • Caudate nucleus
The entorhinal cortex is a critical relay between the hippocampus and neocortex. Its early degeneration in Alzheimer’s disrupts memory circuits.

Question 49

What contributes to synapse loss in Alzheimer’s disease?

  • Myelin degeneration
  • Axonal transport increase
  • Amyloid and tau-induced toxicity
  • Cerebellar demyelination
Both Aβ oligomers and hyperphosphorylated tau contribute to synaptic dysfunction and loss by disrupting membrane potential, intracellular transport, and inflammatory signaling.

Question 50

Which imaging modality shows early Alzheimer’s changes best?

  • CT
  • X-ray
  • MRI
  • PET with FDG
MRI can detect early hippocampal and medial temporal atrophy, making it useful in evaluating early Alzheimer’s before gross clinical decline.

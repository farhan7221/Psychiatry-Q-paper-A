Question 1

Which tool is used to objectively assess sleep-wake cycles via motion detection?

  • Polysomnography
  • EEG
  • Actigraphy
  • EOG
Actigraphy uses a wrist-worn motion sensor to monitor activity and estimate sleep-wake cycles, especially useful for circadian rhythm disorders.

Question 2

What does polysomnography (PSG) typically measure?

  • Body temperature
  • EEG, EOG, EMG, ECG, and respiratory function
  • Blood glucose
  • Light exposure
PSG is the gold standard for sleep studies and includes EEG, EMG, EOG, ECG, oxygen saturation, and respiration to assess sleep stages and disorders.

Question 3

What does 'sleep latency' refer to in PSG analysis?

  • Time between two REM episodes
  • Time from lights out to sleep onset
  • Duration of stage 2 sleep
  • Time between REM and waking
Sleep latency is the time it takes for a person to transition from full wakefulness to sleep after lying down.

Question 4

  • Multiple Sleep Latency Test (MSLT)
  • Dexamethasone suppression test
  • Tilt table test
  • Melatonin assay
MSLT measures how quickly a person falls asleep in a quiet environment during the day, and checks for REM onset within naps.

Question 5

What percentage of adult sleep is spent in NREM stages?

  • 25%
  • 60%
  • 75%
  • 90%
Approximately 75% of adult sleep is non-REM, characterized by physiological slowing and reduced brain activity.

Question 6

Which NREM stage accounts for the highest proportion of sleep?

  • Stage 1
  • Stage 3
  • Stage 2
  • REM
Stage 2 NREM sleep occupies about 45% of the night and features sleep spindles and K complexes.

Question 7

What defines Stage 3 NREM sleep?

  • High-frequency beta waves
  • REM episodes
  • Delta waves <50% of EEG activity
  • Increased muscle tone
Stage 3 is characterized by slow-wave sleep with less than 50% delta activity, deepening sleep and supporting memory consolidation.

Question 8

Which stage of NREM sleep has the lowest physiological activity?

  • Stage 1
  • Stage 2
  • Stage 3
  • Stage 4
Stage 4 is the deepest NREM sleep with more than 50% delta waves and minimum physiological activity (HR, BP, RR).

Question 9

What is a key EEG pattern of REM sleep?

  • Alpha rhythm
  • High amplitude delta waves
  • Low-voltage mixed frequency waves (theta and alpha)
  • Sleep spindles
REM sleep mimics wakefulness with mixed frequency low-voltage EEG activity, reflecting high cortical arousal.

Question 10

How many sleep cycles typically occur during a full night’s sleep?

  • 1–2
  • 4–5
  • 7–8
  • 10–12
A typical adult has 4–5 sleep cycles per night, alternating between NREM and REM phases with increasing REM duration in later cycles.

Question 11

What are sleep spindles associated with?

  • REM sleep
  • Stage 2 NREM sleep
  • Stage 4
  • Wakefulness
Sleep spindles are bursts of 12–14 Hz activity seen in stage 2 NREM sleep, thought to protect sleep and aid in memory.

Question 12

What are K-complexes?

  • Fast EEG waves in REM
  • Muscle twitches during sleep
  • High amplitude slow EEG waves in stage 2 NREM
  • Autonomic spikes
K-complexes are sharp negative deflections followed by a positive wave, usually triggered by external stimuli during stage 2.

Question 13

What defines V waves in sleep?

  • Alpha spikes in REM
  • Sharp, symmetrical slow waves in stage 2 sleep
  • EMG bursts
  • Theta suppression waves
V waves are sharp slow-wave patterns noted during NREM, particularly stage 2, and are symmetrical across hemispheres.

Question 14

Which circuitry mediates K-complex formation?

  • Hippocampo-thalamic loop
  • Cortico-striatal loop
  • Thalamocortical circuitry
  • Reticular activating system
K-complexes are believed to arise from thalamocortical interactions, representing sensory filtering and memory consolidation.

Question 15

Which nucleus is the master regulator of circadian rhythms?

  • VLPO
  • Suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN)
  • Arcuate nucleus
  • Paraventricular nucleus
SCN, located in the anterior hypothalamus, receives light input and synchronizes the biological clock with the environment.

Question 16

Which nucleus promotes sleep initiation?

  • SCN
  • Paraventricular nucleus
  • VLPO (ventrolateral preoptic nucleus)
  • Subthalamic nucleus
VLPO sends inhibitory signals to wake-promoting centers, acting as a 'sleep switch' for initiating and maintaining sleep.

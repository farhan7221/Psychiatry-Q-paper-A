Neurophysiology of Neuroendocrinology Quiz

Prepared by Dr Khalid Farhan

Total Questions: 20

Question 1

Which hormone is secreted by the posterior pituitary and regulates water balance?

  • Oxytocin
  • Vasopressin (ADH)
  • Prolactin
  • Growth hormone
Vasopressin, also called antidiuretic hormone (ADH), promotes water reabsorption in the kidneys and maintains fluid balance.

Question 2

Which hormone stimulates milk ejection during lactation?

  • Oxytocin
  • Prolactin
  • LH
  • FSH
Oxytocin causes contraction of myoepithelial cells in the mammary glands, facilitating milk letdown during breastfeeding.

Question 3

What is the primary regulator of prolactin secretion?

  • TRH
  • Dopamine (PIF)
  • Oxytocin
  • GnRH
Dopamine from the hypothalamus inhibits prolactin secretion; blocking dopamine receptors (e.g., with antipsychotics) increases prolactin.

Question 4

Excess of growth hormone in adults causes which condition?

  • Gigantism
  • Acromegaly
  • Cushing’s syndrome
  • Hyperthyroidism
In adults, GH excess after epiphyseal closure leads to acromegaly (enlarged hands, feet, and facial features).

Question 5

Which hormone stimulates TSH release from the pituitary?

  • CRH
  • GnRH
  • TRH (thyrotropin-releasing hormone)
  • GHRH
TRH from the hypothalamus stimulates the anterior pituitary to secrete TSH, which in turn stimulates the thyroid gland.

Question 6

What is the biologically active form of thyroid hormone?

  • TSH
  • T4
  • T3 (triiodothyronine)
  • rT3
Although T4 is the main circulating form, T3 is more biologically active and is generated by peripheral conversion of T4.

Question 7

Which condition is associated with tachycardia, heat intolerance, and anxiety?

  • Hypothyroidism
  • Hyperthyroidism
  • Addison’s disease
  • Cushing’s syndrome
Hyperthyroidism increases metabolic rate and sympathetic activity, leading to classic signs like palpitations and nervousness.

Question 8

Which mental symptom is common in hypothyroidism?

  • Mania
  • Depression
  • Psychosis
  • Panic attacks
Hypothyroidism often presents with depression, psychomotor slowing, and cognitive difficulties.

Question 9

Which axis regulates the stress response?

  • HPG axis
  • HPT axis
  • HPA axis
  • RAAS
The hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis mediates the stress response via CRH → ACTH → cortisol.

Question 10

Which hormone provides negative feedback to both CRH and ACTH?

  • Aldosterone
  • Cortisol
  • DHEA
  • Epinephrine
Cortisol inhibits its own production by suppressing CRH (hypothalamus) and ACTH (pituitary).

Question 11

What is the main effect of chronic elevated cortisol on the hippocampus?

  • Hypertrophy
  • Atrophy and impaired memory
  • No effect
  • Increased neurogenesis
Prolonged cortisol exposure leads to hippocampal atrophy, impairing learning and memory.

Question 12

Which test assesses the integrity of the HPA axis?

  • Glucose tolerance test
  • Dexamethasone suppression test (DST)
  • ACTH stimulation test
  • Tilt table test
DST measures feedback suppression of cortisol by administering dexamethasone; failure of suppression suggests HPA axis dysfunction.

Question 13

In which disorder is DST non-suppression classically seen?

  • Hypothyroidism
  • Major depression (melancholic type)
  • Cushing’s syndrome
  • Diabetes mellitus
DST non-suppression is a hallmark of Cushing’s syndrome but can also occur in certain psychiatric conditions.

Question 14

What is a key feature of Addison’s disease (primary adrenal insufficiency)?

  • Weight gain
  • Fatigue, hypotension, and hyperpigmentation
  • Tachycardia
  • Increased cortisol
Addison’s involves low cortisol and aldosterone, causing fatigue, low blood pressure, and darkened skin.

Question 15

What gland secretes melatonin?

  • Hypothalamus
  • Pituitary
  • Adrenal cortex
  • Pineal gland
The pineal gland secretes melatonin, regulating circadian rhythms and sleep cycles.

Question 16

What is the main regulator of melatonin synthesis?

  • Body temperature
  • Blood glucose
  • Light-dark cycle
  • Cortisol
Melatonin secretion is stimulated by darkness and inhibited by light, allowing adaptation to environmental lighting.

Question 17

Which neurotransmitter is the precursor for melatonin?

  • Dopamine
  • GABA
  • Serotonin
  • Acetylcholine
Serotonin is converted to melatonin in the pineal gland via enzymatic steps involving serotonin-N-acetyltransferase.

Question 18

What is a common calcified structure seen in the pineal gland with age?

  • Pineocytoma
  • Amyloid plaque
  • Corpora arenacea (brain sand)
  • Pituicytes
The pineal gland accumulates calcium deposits known as corpora arenacea (brain sand), especially with aging.

Question 19

Which pituitary hormone increases during early morning sleep?

  • GH
  • Prolactin
  • TSH
  • ACTH
Prolactin secretion peaks during sleep, especially in early morning hours, supporting reproductive functions.

Question 20

Which two hormones rise during slow-wave sleep?

  • ACTH and TSH
  • Growth hormone (GH) and somatostatin (SST)
  • Cortisol and aldosterone
  • LH and FSH
GH release surges during deep (slow-wave) sleep, accompanied by increased SST, supporting growth and repair.
